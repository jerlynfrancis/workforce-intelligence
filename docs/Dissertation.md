# 3.0: Methodology

## 3.1 Research Approach

Given the applied nature of this business simulation, I have selected a pragmatic mixed methods framework (Abbas Tashakkori and Teddlie, 2010). This would help us capture both the statistical realities of our performance while confirming the strategic reasoning made behind key decisions. In my capacity as the Technology & Engineering Executive at Lumon Nova, I had the opportunity to observe how our choices played out over a decade of market competition simulated using the European BEV.

Our research design basically follows what both Creswell and Plano Clark (2017) and (Abbas Tashakkori and Teddlie, 2010) described as the integration of quantitative metrics with managerial insights that are qualitative in nature. The difference being, the management in question at Lumon Nova, was just my own decision-making process under simulated conditions. I treated this as an embedded case study of the company's evolution through ten different annual cycles, aligning with the mixed methods framework mentioned by Abbas Tashakkori and Teddlie (2010) both qualitative and quantitative methods in order to understand how strategies often unfold within organizational functions as time passes.

My analytical sequence was very straightforward: first, I extracted patterns from the data, from market share trends, productivity metrics, and spending done on automations, to warranty related costs, and staffing levels from the annual reports. Then I delved into the why behind the inflection points, mainly examining decisions like the substantial £300m automation investment in Year 8. I also went through the workforce restructuring in the final year that allowed us to maintain our preferred five-model production while reducing headcount in over a thousand positions.

Rather than getting bogged down by complex statistical modelling, I focused on practical analysis using techniques that matched the simulation's scope. This included trend analysis of our key performance indicators (KPIs), chronological mapping of all major decisions, and systematic comparison with our simulated competitors. This approach allowed me to

triangulate between what the numbers showed and what went behind the reasoning on certain specific choices.

## 3.3 Data Analysis Methods

For the quantitative analysis, we used descriptive, and decision-relevant techniques. First, trend analysis mapped KPIs across all 10 years. This includes the market-share trajectories, automation cadence, workforce levels, productivity contrasts, and warranty ranges. Time ordered displays were used to support a light time-series examination focused only on direction and magnitude rather than the statistical inference (Otexts.com, 2021).

Secondly, we benchmarked simulated competitors to compare Lumon Nova's automation intensity, productivity, quality outcomes (like zero recalls and warranty levels), and market-share performance against. Benchmarks were drawn using Market Research and Production reports and then interpreted using established principles of measurement that are generally used for identifying gaps and improving priorities (Ayşenur Erdil and Hikmet Erbıyık, 2019).

Qualitative analysis employed reflexive thematic analysis (Using thematic analysis in psychology, 2025) on our decision logs and meeting notes. Initial coding found recurring themes in engineering; be it automation leadership, cross platform integration, demand supply alignment, workforce optimisation, or quality management. Codes were grouped into a set of higher order themes that explained the evolution from foundation to expansion.

The emphasis was on reasoning and trade offs; so no inter coder reliability metrics were used. Mixed-methods integration occurred in three different ways. First, an alignment matrix of performance decisions linked yearly indicators to documented engineering actions to check their coherence. ## 3.4 Limitations and Ethical Considerations

This methodology is bounded by our business simulation. ### 4.4.3 Aligning Demand - Supply and Profit Margin Recovery processes

Financially one of the most significant pressures arose from inability to forecast Demand and Supply alignments which led to excessive production. Drastic measures such as tied up working capital and increased carried over costs were taken, which delayed the ROIs of heavy investments in automation. Instead of generating scale economies, dead stocks affected gross margins. This confirmed Brynjolfsson, Hitt and Kim (2011) claims of poor predictive alignments affecting ROI's, even in technologically advanced firms. Measures were taken to tackle the major challenges Lumon Nova faced, such as adopting leaner production plans and cost-conscious price adjustments. This helped rebuild demand and supply balance which showcased financial resilience requiring flexible forecasting tools and price adjustment strategies (Gordon, Goldfarb and Li, 2012; Van Heerde et al., 2013).

### 4.4.4 Workforce Optimization and Trade- Offs

The 10th Year rebuilding plans resulted in saving labor costs which confirmed the financial reasoning behind previous automation investments. The remaining workforce capital was sufficient to meet production targets (demand-supply analysis based) across all five models, hence maintaining profit margins despite lowered production volumes. The redundancies however still created reputational liabilities ,especially cause workforce imbalance damaged social sustainability goals of Lumon Nova in comparison to competitors .This by its nature acts as a financial trade off, as while short term costs were reduced but at the risk of long term value creation goals being affected due to reputational damage .This showcases how firms that prioritize workforce stability often earn stronger reputational and long term financial performance (Ramos and María Eugenia Ruiz-Gálvez, 2024) compared to firms that don't.

# 5.0 Recommendations and Reflection

## 5.1 Strategic Recommendations

### 5.1.1 Future Engineering Strategies

The next phase of Lumon Nova's engineering journey needs to build upon its automation and quality maintenance leadership. To truly elevate manufacturing capabilities, automation should go beyond the current limits of 20–25% allocation, particularly in high volume models, by incorporating Industry 4.0 tech such as digital twins, IoT enabled monitoring, and even cyber physical systems. These help in enabling real-time optimisation while supporting a zero-defect assurance (Mounia Achouch et al., 2022).

Sustainability is something we consider important in this phase. With over 66.36% of customers expressing interest in our carbon capture engines, this tech alongside hydrogen IC systems and ultra-low-emission systems can benefit highly from priority commercialisation. Decarbonising the supply chain would offer us a very unique first-mover advantage in a industry that's already facing tight environmental regulations (Månsson et al., 2023). Finally, deploying our AI driven predictive maintenance systems projects the ability to optimise energy efficiency, reduce downtime, and even forecast equipment and development failures. This helps align engineering with cost management and sustainability (Ülge Taş, 2024; Mounia Achouch et al., 2022). Together, these initiatives should ensure Lumon Nova remains a leader in innovation, quality, and showcase excellent environmental performance and set an example.

### 5.1.2 Decision-Making Process Improvements

We have made quite a success of large-scale commitments such as the £300m automation upgrade in Year 8, but despite that Lumon Nova's decision-making process requires refinement. Real-time competitive intelligence systems need to be invested in, and embedded to monitor competitors' automation, pricing, or R&D strategies moving us to a more data-driven decision playbook (Mounia Achouch et al., 2022).

Cross-functional frameworks could be another interesting angle for improvement. This ensures engineering decisions are synchronised with finance and marketing to avoid mismatches like

44

the Sprint surplus of over 66k units in Year 8. Large capital investments should be evaluated through structured risk assessment frameworks, for e.g., a stage gate or real options analysis, to help reduce unnecessary exposure.

Finally, scenario planning tools could be used to stress-test our new strategies such as workforce restructuring or automation tiering under variations of different demand and regulatory conditions. This directly adds to our resilience and agility, abiding with best practice in markets as uncertain as automotive (Månsson et al., 2023).

### 5.1.3 Simulation Enhancement Recommendations

The simulation experience could be upgraded by simply integrating tech that support-decisions. Something like an analytics dashboard showing real time KPIs such as inventory, bottlenecks, or the success of a feature adoption would improve visibility and reduce the current lag in decision-making (Mounia Achouch et al., 2022).

Providing greater transparency on the strategies of our competitors, focusing on automation and R&D investments, would simulate far more realistic benchmarking conditions. Similarly, modelling R&D timelines and probabilities of success would better reflect industry practice. It is well known how high risk technologies like ceramic brakes often face longer cycles and uncertain outcomes (Mounia Achouch et al., 2022).

Finally, the simulation should incorporate the dynamic market conditions for e.g., spontaneous emissions legislation or changing customer preferences challenging participants to adapt long term strategies under sheer uncertainty (Ülge Taş, 2024).

### 5.2.1 Key Learning Outcomes

Uncertainty is at the core of this business and understanding how to take quick decisions with such risk was one of the most important lessons throughout this dissertation. Committing over £620m to go all in on automation between Years 4 to 6 required confidence in long term productivity gains, in the midst of margin pressures. This mirrored real world industry

This illustrates the need to see engineering as part of an interconnected corporate system, rather than a separate division it's often treated as (Mounia Achouch et al., 2022).

The simulation led to development of skills directly applicable to the automotive industry. Second, financial analysis skills were taken to a new level. Evaluating ROI on automation investments, such as the £300m Year 8 upgrade, meant the need for balancing long term productivity with those immediate margin impacts. This reflects how real industries have a very capital intensive structure where cost benefit analysis underpins even survival (Månsson et al., 2023).
